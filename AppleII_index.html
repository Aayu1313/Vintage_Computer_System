Vintage Computing Systems

BY Aayushi, Holly, Viktorija, Mariana

Apple II (1976)

The Apple II launched in 1977, was a true trailblazer in personal computing. Designed by Steve Wozniak and marketed by Steve Jobs, it was a follow-up to the Apple I—but this time, it was built with a broader vision: bringing computers into everyday life. Unlike its predecessor, which was more of a DIY kit, the Apple II was a fully assembled machine, ready for use straight out of the box.This shift was key in making computers more accessible to a wider audience.

The Apple II stood out for its emphasis on user-friendly design, making computing more accessible to everyday users.It has a built-in keyboard and support for colour graphics, rare at the time.It ran BASIC, a simple programming language that lets users write their programs without needing deep technical knowledge.

Storage was initially handled through cassette tapes, but soon, Apple introduced the Disk II system, bringing floppy disks into the mix. This was revolutionary, as it assembled loading and saving programs much faster and more reliably.

The Apple II was built to be adaptable, offering a range of features to suit various needs.It had multiple expansion slots, meaning users could add memory, graphics cards, and exact specialised peripherals. This ability to customise the computer helped extend its lifespan unknown beyond that of many other machines of the time.

While businesses began adopting computers, the Apple II carved a niche in schools and homes.Schools saw its potential as an educational tool, and before long, it became a staple in classrooms across the United States and beyond.Meanwhile, game developers embraced the Apple II capabilities, leading to an explosion of interactive entertainment.Early classics like Oregon Trail and Ultima made their mark, proving that computers were not just for work—they could be fun, too.The Apple II, not just set the foundation for Apple as a company; it helped define what personal computing could be.

They showed the world that computers were not just for hobbyists or businesses; they could be an integral part of everyday life.The technique choices, like expandability and colour graphics, influenced the future of computing in significant ways.

At a time when personal computers were still a novelty, the Apple II brought them into the mainstream. It laid the groundwork for the Macintosh, modern PCs, and even today sleek laptops and smartphones.In many ways, it was the spark that ignited the digital revolution.
